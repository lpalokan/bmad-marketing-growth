# Storyline Review (SR)

<instructions>
Critique a submitted storyline against the three-act structure.
Reference the user's actual content (quote their words back when
critiquing); explain framework concepts only when invoking them.
</instructions>

<process>

## 1. Acknowledge and intake

Confirm what was received: a draft outline, slide titles, an existing
deck's structure, a narrative summary, etc. If the input is ambiguous,
ask up to three targeted questions in a single numbered list before
reviewing:

1. Target audience (without this, the audience-as-protagonist principle is unverifiable)
2. Presentation length (governs Act II depth expectations)
3. Desired outcome / decision the audience must make (without this, Act III can't be assessed)

If the submission is a topic list rather than a narrative, explain
why a narrative is needed before reviewing, and offer to run BP
(Build Presentation) instead from source artifacts.

If the goal is **not persuasive** (status update, training module),
say so and note that the three-act structure is optimised for
persuasion. Offer to apply it anyway or to recommend a lighter
adaptation.

## 2. Map the submitted storyline to BBP slots

Build a mapping table — populate from the user's actual content:

| BBP Element             | Present? | Current content (or "missing") | Assessment |
|-------------------------|----------|--------------------------------|------------|
| Hook                    | …        | …                              | …          |
| Relevance               | …        | …                              | …          |
| Challenge (Point A)     | …        | …                              | …          |
| Desire (Point B)        | …        | …                              | …          |
| Map / Call to Action    | …        | …                              | …          |
| Anchor 1                | …        | …                              | …          |
| Anchor 2                | …        | …                              | …          |
| Anchor 3                | …        | …                              | …          |
| Act III Resolution      | …        | …                              | …          |

## 3. Diagnose (write into Findings by act)

For each act, name specific issues with reasoning:

- Where is dramatic tension weak or absent? Is Challenge ≠ Desire, or
  do they collapse into the same thing?
- Is the audience cast as the main character? Or is the
  presenter / company / product the focus?
- Does Act II follow a clean 3-anchor structure with anchors ordered
  most-to-least important?
- Are headlines complete sentences in active voice? Quote the worst
  offender back to the user.
- Is Act III a real resolution and decision, or is it a
  "Thank you / Questions?" slide?

Distinguish **structural problems** (wrong shape) from **content
problems** (right shape, weak words).

## 4. Provide structured feedback

Use this default response structure:

### Summary verdict (2–4 sentences)
Direct assessment: which acts work, which need most work, and the
single highest-impact change.

### Mapping table
The table from step 2.

### Findings by act
Three short sections — Act I, Act II, Act III — with specific issues
and reasoning. Quote the user's own words when critiquing specific lines.

### Suggested rewrites
Concrete proposed headlines and structural changes. Show
before/after pairs where useful. Include at least one rewritten
headline so the user sees the standard in practice.

### Open questions or recommended next step
Either targeted questions needed to refine further, or the specific
next iteration to send back (e.g. "Send the revised Act I headlines
and I'll review the pair").

## 5. Quality standards (self-check before sending)

A good review:
- References the user's actual content, not generic theory
- Identifies the single largest problem before listing smaller ones
- Provides at least one rewritten headline as proof of the standard
- Distinguishes structural from content problems
- Avoids restating theory the user didn't ask for; explains a concept
  only when invoking it in feedback

A poor review (avoid):
- Generic rules without connection to the submission
- "Do X" without showing how
- Treats every issue as equally important
- Praise that doesn't identify what specifically works and why

## 6. Constraints

- Do not invent missing content. If Act II anchors are absent, ask
  for them or flag them as missing; do not fabricate the user's
  argument.
- Do not redesign slides, visuals, or graphics — storyline scope only.
- Do not impose the 3×3×3 hierarchy rigidly when the presentation
  length doesn't warrant it; for short talks, anchors alone may
  suffice. Note explicitly when relaxing the rule.
- Do not reference editions or pages of any source book as authority.
  Apply the framework directly.

## 7. Output

Write the review into `{output_folder}/work/{presentation-id}/v{n}-review.md`
if operating under a brief; otherwise hand back inline and offer to
save with SM.

</process>
